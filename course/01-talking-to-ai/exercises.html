Exercises

Three exercises you can run right now in any chatbot. Each one is designed to make you feel the difference in output quality when you apply the principles — not just understand them in the abstract.

Before you start

Open your chatbot of choice in another tab (ChatGPT, Claude.ai, or Gemini — any will do). No setup required. For each exercise, try writing your own version first before expanding the sample answer. The point isn't to match the example word for word; it's to notice what information you left out, and what difference that made to the output.

Exercise 1 · Write a work brief Principles 1 & 2

The following request is typical of how most people start with AI — it reads like a Google search. Your task is to rewrite it as a proper work brief: who the AI is, who you are and what's at stake, what you actually want produced, the purpose behind the request, and any constraints on the output.

The vague request:

What should I focus on for my consulting interview prep?

Your task: Rewrite this as a full work brief using Principles 1 and 2. Aim for 8–12 lines. When you run both versions back-to-back in your chatbot, the gap in output quality should be obvious.

Show one possible answer → 
You are an experienced management consulting recruiter who has
interviewed hundreds of candidates for strategy roles at McKinsey,
BCG, and Bain.

I'm a second-year MBA student with a background in marketing
analytics. I have a first-round case interview at BCG in 10 days.
I've done 15 practice cases. I'm consistently strong on structure
and math, but my interviewers say my recommendations feel generic —
I don't "so what" enough at the end.

Given that specific weakness, build me a focused 10-day prep plan.
Each day should have one targeted activity (30–60 min), a brief
explanation of why that activity addresses the recommendation
synthesis gap, and one observable signal that tells me I've done
it well.

Do not include general interview advice I already know. Everything
should be targeted to the "generic conclusion" problem.

What this prompt does that the original doesn't: it establishes the AI's expertise (Principle 1), gives the AI the context it needs to make good judgment calls — MBA year, background, timeline, specific weakness — and explains the purpose so the AI knows what to optimize for (Principle 2). The last line cuts the AI's most common failure mode on this task: padding with generic tips.

Exercise 2 · Make the analysis auditable Principle 4

You are a summer analyst helping your manager evaluate two software vendors for a new project management contract. You've gathered the specs below. If you just ask "which vendor should we go with?", you get a conclusion — but not something you can present or defend. Your task is to rewrite the prompt so the AI walks through its reasoning first, producing an output you could actually drop into a recommendation memo.

Vendor specs:

Vendor A
  Annual cost:       $45,000
  Implementation:    6 weeks
  CRM integration:   Partial — requires custom development work
  User rating:       4.2 / 5  (800+ reviews)
  References:        3 provided, all positive
  Contract term:     1 year, renewable annually

Vendor B
  Annual cost:       $32,000
  Implementation:    12 weeks
  CRM integration:   Native (out of the box)
  User rating:       3.9 / 5  (200 reviews)
  References:        1 provided
  Contract term:     2-year minimum commitment

The vague request:

Based on this, which vendor should we go with?

Your task: Rewrite this so the AI reasons step by step before concluding. The output should be something you could present to your manager — with logic they can question — not just an answer you're passing along.

Show one possible answer → 
I'm recommending a project management software vendor to my
manager. Before giving a recommendation, work through the
following:

1. For a mid-size company with an existing CRM, what are the
   three or four criteria that should drive this decision?
   For each one, briefly explain why it matters in practice.

2. Evaluate each vendor against those criteria using the specs
   below. If any spec is ambiguous or missing information you'd
   want before being confident, flag it.

3. Give a recommendation. Then name the single assumption
   underlying it that, if wrong, would flip the answer.

Vendor specs: [paste specs above]

Why this works in practice: step 1 surfaces the criteria before any scoring happens, so you can push back if the AI misses something your manager cares about (say, the cost of the custom CRM work, or the risk of a 2-year lock-in). The last line — "what assumption would flip this?" — is one of the most useful things you can ask on any recommendation task. It tells you exactly where to probe.

Exercise 3 · The follow-up loop Principles 3 & 5 · produces a real artifact

This exercise has two parts. In Part A you'll run a deliberately underspecified first prompt to get a rough output. In Part B you'll write a targeted follow-up that turns it into something professionally usable — without starting over.

The raw data:

customer feedback - dec
- "loved the new dashboard - too slow tho" (acme corp, enterprise)
- buyflow keeps breaking on mobile -- 3rd complaint this week (smb)
- "support was great, fixed in 20 min, John helped" mid-market
- the integration with salesforce STILL doesn't sync custom fields.
  priority?? (enterprise, $$$)
- price too high vs competitors, considering switch (smb, 12mo contract)
- new dashboard is gorgeous but where did the export button go (mid)
- mobile is unusable. seriously. (multiple tickets)
- love everything, no notes (enterprise, expansion opp)

Part A — run this weak prompt first:

Analyze this customer feedback and tell me the main themes.

You will get something back — a list of themes, some bullet points, maybe a paragraph. It will be vaguely accurate and professionally useless. Don't edit it or start over. Read it, note specifically what's missing or wrong, and move to Part B.

Part B — write a targeted follow-up:

Without starting a new conversation, write a follow-up message that fixes the specific weaknesses of what you got. Your follow-up should name what worked, specify what needs to change, and give the format you actually need. Aim for a Markdown table suitable for a PM update or board appendix.

Show one possible follow-up → 
Good start on identifying the areas. A few targeted changes:

1. The themes are too broad to be actionable. Instead of "product
   issues", break it into the specific problem areas that appear
   in the data: performance, mobile, integrations, UX, pricing,
   support, expansion.

2. Reorganize everything as a Markdown table with these columns:
     Theme | Mentions | Sentiment | Severity | Example quote
   For severity: high = revenue at risk or repeated complaint,
   medium = friction but no stated switching intent,
   low = minor or one-off.

3. After the table, add two sentences: what is the single highest-
   priority issue to address, and what's the cost of not fixing it?
   Use only evidence from the feedback — no speculation.

Notice what this follow-up does: it names what the first output got right ("good start on identifying the areas"), tells the AI exactly what needs to change, shows the format (column names and severity definitions), and adds a new deliverable at the end — all without restarting. That last paragraph is the grounding principle in action: asking for a conclusion anchored in the evidence, not generated from thin air.

What you should notice

The follow-up in Part B probably did more to improve the output than any first-pass prompt could have — because you were responding to what you actually got, not what you imagined. This is the core skill. The first prompt opens the conversation; the follow-up is where the real work happens.

Reflection questions

After completing all three exercises, sit with these for a minute before moving on.

  • In Exercise 1, which element of the work brief had the biggest impact on output quality — the role, the context, the purpose, or the constraints? Would your answer change for a different kind of task?
  • In Exercise 2, did the AI's criteria list match what you would have chosen? Did it weight the CRM integration cost or the 2-year lock-in the way you'd expect a manager to? If not, how would you correct that in a follow-up?
  • In Exercise 3, how much better was the second output than the first? What would have happened if you'd just rewritten the original prompt instead of following up?

What's next

You've seen what's possible inside a chatbot. The limitation is that everything you just did was one-off: close the tab and it's gone. The next module shows what changes when the AI can read files on your computer, run code, and operate across many steps without you narrating each one. That's where the leverage starts to compound.
