Exercises

Three exercises you can do right now in any chatbot (ChatGPT, Claude.ai, Gemini — pick one). No setup required. The third one produces a real artifact you can use.

Before you start

Open your chatbot of choice in another tab. Don't read ahead to the answer keys — try each exercise first, then compare. The point isn't to get the "right" answer; it's to feel the difference between an underspecified prompt and a well-specified one.

Exercise 1 · Apply Pattern 1 (give it a role)

The vague request: "Help me decide whether to take a summer internship at a startup or a big consulting firm."

Your task: Rewrite this prompt to give the AI a specific role and the constraints it needs to give you useful output. Aim for 4–6 lines.

Show one possible answer → 
You are a career advisor who has counseled hundreds of Business School Student
students. I am a first-year Business School Student deciding between two summer
internships: (1) a Series B startup as a product manager, and
(2) a top-tier strategy consulting firm in their tech practice.
My long-term goal is to start a company in 5-7 years.

Ask me three questions whose answers would most change your
recommendation. Don't give a recommendation yet.

The key moves: assigned role, concrete options, explicit long-term goal, and asking for the most decision-relevant questions rather than a premature recommendation.

Exercise 2 · Apply Pattern 2 (show examples)

The vague request: "Summarize these five news articles for me."

Your task: Rewrite this prompt with an explicit format example so all five summaries will be directly comparable.

Show one possible answer → 
Summarize each of the five articles below in this exact format:

  Article: [headline]
  Source: [publication]
  Main claim (1 sentence): ...
  Evidence cited: [bullet list of 1-3 items, or "none"]
  Counter-perspective acknowledged: yes / no
  My take in 1 sentence on whether the claim is well-supported: ...

If a field can't be filled from the article, write "not in
source". Don't invent information.

Exercise 3 · Produce a real artifact

This one's different — you'll end this exercise with a file you can use. Copy the messy data below into your chatbot, write a prompt that uses at least two of the four patterns, and ask for the output as a clean Markdown table.

The messy data:

customer feedback - dec
- "loved the new dashboard - too slow tho" (acme corp, enterprise)
- buyflow keeps breaking on mobile -- 3rd complaint this week (smb)
- "support was great, fixed in 20 min, John helped" mid-market
- the integration with salesforce STILL doesn't sync custom fields. priority?? (enterprise, $$$)
- price too high vs competitors, considering switch (smb, 12mo contract)
- new dashboard is gorgeous but where did the export button go (mid)
- mobile is unusable. seriously. (multiple tickets)
- love everything, no notes (enterprise, expansion opp)

What "good" looks like: a Markdown table with columns for segment, theme, sentiment, and severity — sortable in your head, easy to drop into a PM update or a board appendix.

Show a prompt that works → 
You are a customer success analyst preparing a monthly themes
report. Below is raw, informally-written feedback from December.

For each piece of feedback, extract:
  - Segment (enterprise / mid-market / smb / unknown)
  - Theme (one of: performance, mobile, integrations, pricing,
    UX, support, expansion). If none fit, write "other" and add a
    short label.
  - Sentiment (positive / negative / mixed)
  - Severity (low / medium / high) based on revenue exposure and
    frequency

Output as a Markdown table with those four columns. After the
table, list the top two themes by count and any single piece of
feedback that names a specific dollar risk.

Do not invent information that isn't in the feedback. If
segment or another field is missing, write "unknown".
What you should notice

By the end of Exercise 3 you have a real artifact — a themes table — produced in less time than it would have taken to format a spreadsheet by hand. That's the floor of vibecoding. Everything in the rest of the course raises the ceiling.

What's next

You've now seen what's possible inside a chatbot. The catch is that everything you did was a one-off — close the tab and it's gone. The next module shows what changes when the AI can read files on your computer, run code, and operate on its own across many steps. That's where the real leverage starts.
