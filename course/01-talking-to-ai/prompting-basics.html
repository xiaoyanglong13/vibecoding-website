Prompting basics

Five principles — drawn from Anthropic, OpenAI, and peer-reviewed research — that separate outputs you can use from outputs you have to fix. Each one changes how you think about AI, not just what you type.

Most people prompt like they Google: a few words that gesture at what they want. The AI responds, and what comes back is the average of everything it knows about that vague request — technically accurate, professionally useless.

The gap between a weak prompt and a strong one isn't mystical. It is, in essence, the gap between a vague assignment and a well-written work brief. The five principles below address that gap systematically. They are grounded in documented research, not folklore — chain-of-thought prompting, for instance, has been shown to improve accuracy on complex reasoning tasks by 20–50%. Master these and you'll spend less time editing AI output and more time using it.

The five principles

Principle 1 · Write a work brief, not a wish

The most common prompting mistake is treating a prompt like a search query. A search query works because a search engine finds documents that match your keywords. A prompt works differently: you are handing off a job to a capable but context-free contractor. Your goal is to eliminate ambiguity about the task, the audience, the constraints, and what a good output looks like.

Anthropic describes this as the brilliant new hire test: imagine showing your prompt to a smart, motivated person on their first day who has zero background on your company, your project, or your preferences. Would they have everything they need to do the job well? If not, what's missing? That missing information belongs in the prompt.

Before:

Analyze our customer churn data.

After:

You are a strategy analyst helping the VP of Customer Success
prepare for a board update. Below is 12 months of monthly churn
data, broken out by segment (SMB, mid-market, enterprise).

Identify the two most significant trends. For each trend:
  - State what the data shows
  - Suggest the most plausible explanation
  - Name one question you'd want answered before drawing
    a firm conclusion

Format: two paragraphs, no bullet points. The audience is
non-technical executives.

The "after" version specifies role, audience, task structure, and output format. Every element you leave unspecified is a place where the AI has to guess — and its guess is unlikely to match what you needed.

Principle 2 · Explain the why, not just the what

Most prompts describe a task. The best prompts also explain why the task matters — and this changes the output more than most people expect.

When the AI understands the purpose behind a request, it can make intelligent judgment calls on things you didn't think to specify: what to emphasize for this audience, which caveats to include, what's worth flagging even if you didn't ask. Anthropic's own documentation puts it plainly: "Providing context or motivation behind your instructions … can help Claude better understand your goals and deliver more targeted responses. Claude is smart enough to generalize from the explanation."

The practical implication: a sentence or two of purpose often does more work than adding another paragraph of instructions.

Before:

Summarize this vendor contract.

After:

Summarize this vendor contract. I'm a first-year associate
preparing a briefing for a senior partner who needs to decide
in the next two hours whether to recommend signing.

Flag any clauses that deviate from our standard terms or that
carry material risk. Keep the summary under 300 words.

The second prompt tells the AI what "good" looks like from the reader's perspective. That changes what the AI includes, what it emphasizes, and what it flags — often more than any additional instruction would.

Principle 3 · Show an example — don't describe the format

If you have a specific format in mind, show it. Don't describe it.

This is the single most reliable technique in the prompt engineering literature. Academically, it is called few-shot prompting. The intuition: examples are higher-bandwidth than descriptions. A single well-chosen example eliminates an enormous amount of ambiguity that even a careful paragraph of instructions cannot. Research consistently shows that three to five good examples outperform elaborate format specifications.

If you have prior outputs you're happy with — a memo you wrote, a summary from a previous week — paste one in as your example. That is almost always worth the effort.

Before:

Summarize each sales call in a structured way.

After:

Summarize each sales call in the following format:

  Call: [date] with [company name]
  Stage: [prospect / active / at-risk / won / lost]
  Key concern raised: [1-2 sentences]
  Next action: [what we committed to, with owner and date]
  Risk flag: [yes/no — if yes, 1 sentence explaining why]

Example of a well-done summary:

  Call: March 14 with Thornton Logistics
  Stage: active
  Key concern raised: Their IT team needs three weeks to review
    integration requirements before they can commit.
  Next action: Jamie will send technical specs by March 17.
  Risk flag: Yes — if specs aren't approved by March 28,
    deal likely slips past quarter-end.

One concrete example does more work than two paragraphs of formatting instructions. It also makes the output enforceable: you can scan a hundred summaries quickly because they all look the same.

Principle 4 · Ask for reasoning, not just conclusions

For any task that involves analysis, a recommendation, or a decision, ask the AI to show its reasoning before it gives you the answer.

This technique — chain-of-thought prompting in the research — improves accuracy on complex analytical tasks by 20–50%, according to published benchmarks. But accuracy is not the main reason to use it in a business context.

The more important reason is auditability. When the AI shows its work, you can evaluate the logic rather than just the conclusion. You can challenge an assumption, notice a missing variable, or catch a misread of the data. If the AI delivers only a conclusion, you have no leverage to interrogate it. And when you are using AI output to inform a real decision, the ability to interrogate it is not optional.

Before:

Based on this market data, what should our pricing strategy be?

After:

Based on the market data below, what should our pricing
strategy be?

Before giving your recommendation, work through:
  1. What does the data suggest about price sensitivity
     in each customer segment?
  2. What are the most relevant competitive reference points?
  3. What are the main trade-offs between the likely options?

Then give a recommendation. Finally, name the single assumption
that, if wrong, would most change your answer.

The AI's answer is now reviewable at every step. If you disagree with how it read price sensitivity in the SMB segment, you can say so — and get a substantive revision, not a reshuffled version of the same conclusion.

Why this matters beyond accuracy

In a professional setting, you are accountable for the decisions you make with AI output — not the AI. Prompting for reasoning is how you maintain that accountability. An output you can audit is an output you can defend.

Principle 5 · Follow up — don't start over

The first output from a prompt is almost never the final answer. It is a first draft.

Most people respond to an imperfect output by rewriting the prompt from scratch. This is almost always the wrong move. A targeted follow-up — redirecting a specific flaw, asking for more depth on one section, telling the AI what worked and what didn't — is faster and typically produces a better result. The AI retains the full context of the conversation and can build on what's already working.

Think of it like working with an analyst: you wouldn't reassign the project after every first draft. You'd give feedback. The same instinct applies.

The most productive pattern: generate → critique specifically → redirect.

Weak follow-up (restarts the task):

Write a better version of the financial analysis section.

Strong follow-up (builds on what's there):

The first three paragraphs work well. The financial analysis
section is too generic — sharpen it using the actual figures
from the Q3 report I pasted above (the revenue variance table
on page 4 is especially relevant). Also cut the opening
disclaimer paragraph; the reader doesn't need it.

Specific critique outperforms vague instructions by a wide margin. "Make it better" gives the AI no signal. "Here is what is wrong and here is what you should do instead" gives it everything it needs.

The corollary: don't over-engineer the first prompt trying to get a perfect result in one shot. A focused first response at 80% that you can redirect in two follow-ups will almost always beat a three-paragraph prompt that tries to anticipate everything.

Put them together

Most strong prompts use three or four of these principles at once. Here is a compound example for a strategy task:

You are a strategy associate at a consulting firm. Your client
is a mid-market SaaS company evaluating whether to expand into
the enterprise segment. I need a first-cut analysis to bring
to a partner meeting tomorrow morning — not a final recommendation,
but a structured way to frame the decision.

(Principle 1: role + audience + task defined; Principle 2: purpose stated)

Using the market data and internal metrics below, work through:
  1. What are the two strongest arguments for expansion?
  2. What are the two strongest arguments against?
  3. What are the two or three things we'd need to know to
     resolve the uncertainty?

(Principle 4: reasoning first)

Use this structure for each argument:
  Argument: [1-sentence claim]
  Evidence: [what in the data supports this]
  Caveat: [what would weaken or complicate it]

(Principle 3: format shown, not described)

[market data and internal metrics pasted below]

After reviewing the output, a good follow-up might be: "The competitive analysis in argument 2 is underdeveloped. Pull out the pricing data from the enterprise benchmarks section and rebuild that argument around the margin implications." That is Principle 5 in action — targeted, specific, building forward.

A note on grounding

All five principles above are about how you structure a request. There is one more factor that deserves its own mention: what you give the AI to work with.

AI models perform significantly better when you supply the source material rather than asking them to rely on general knowledge. Paste in the actual contract, the actual meeting notes, the actual data. This is called grounding — you are anchoring the AI's response to specific information rather than letting it synthesize from training data alone. It substantially reduces hallucination (confident-sounding fabrication) and substantially increases relevance. When precision matters, always give the AI the documents. Don't ask it to guess what they say.

Pitfalls at this stage

  • Describing format instead of showing it. If you have a template, paste it. If you have a prior example, use it. No amount of verbal description matches the clarity of one well-chosen example.
  • Accepting the first output. The first response is a draft. Follow up with specific critique rather than accepting a mediocre answer or starting over from scratch.
  • Using AI conclusions without checking the reasoning. For any decision that matters, ask for the reasoning first. An output you cannot audit is an output you cannot responsibly act on.
  • Omitting the why. Telling the AI the purpose behind a task is not throat-clearing — it is one of the highest- leverage things you can put in a prompt. Don't skip it.
  • Over-prompting on simple tasks. These principles scale with task complexity. A one-paragraph email does not need a five-element brief. Match prompt depth to task stakes.
