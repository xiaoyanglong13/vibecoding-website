Prompting basics

Four patterns that turn 80% of vague requests into useful outputs. Each one comes with a before/after example.

The four patterns

You don't need to memorize a hundred "prompt engineering" tricks. Four patterns cover most of what a Business School Student needs. Once they're automatic, the rest is just practice.

Pattern 1 · Give the AI a role

The AI behaves differently depending on whose shoes you ask it to step into. Start your prompt by telling it who it is for this task.

Before:

Look at this customer feedback and tell me what's wrong.

After:

You are a senior product manager at a B2B SaaS company.
You are reviewing customer feedback to decide what to put on
next quarter's roadmap. Read the feedback below and identify the
three themes that affect the most enterprise customers. For each
theme, estimate severity (low/medium/high) and suggest one
follow-up question I should ask the affected accounts.

The "before" version gives you a generic complaint summary. The "after" version gives you something usable in a roadmap meeting.

Pattern 2 · Show examples of what you want

If you can describe the output format with an example, do it. Examples beat descriptions almost every time.

Before:

Summarize each of these meeting notes in a concise way.

After:

Summarize each of these meeting notes in the following format:

  Meeting: [date] with [attendees]
  Decision made: [1 sentence, or "none"]
  Owner: [name, or "unassigned"]
  Next check-in: [date, or "not scheduled"]

If any field is missing from the notes, write "not specified".
Do not invent information.

The "after" version is enforceable — you can scan a hundred summaries quickly because they all look the same. The "before" version gives you a hundred slightly different paragraphs.

Pattern 3 · State what you don't want

AIs default to being helpful in ways that often aren't useful. Tell it what to skip.

Before:

Write a one-paragraph executive summary of this report.

After:

Write a one-paragraph executive summary of this report.
Do not start with "This report..." or "In summary..."
Do not include any caveats about needing more information.
Do not use the words "leverage", "synergy", or "robust".
If something is unclear in the source, ask me — don't guess.

You've now eliminated four common failure modes in one pass.

Pattern 4 · Ask the AI to think first, answer second

For anything analytical, separate "thinking" from "output".

Before:

Which of these three vendors should we pick?

After:

I need to choose one of three vendors (details below).
First, list the criteria a reasonable buyer would weigh.
Then score each vendor against each criterion (1-5).
Then state your recommendation and the one criterion that
matters most for this decision. If two vendors tie on the
top criterion, name the tiebreaker.

The "after" version is auditable. You can disagree with the criteria, the scores, or the tiebreaker — and the disagreement is concrete enough to act on.

Put them together

Most good prompts use two or three of these at once. Here's a compound example:

You are a marketing analyst preparing a board update. (role)

Below is our last 12 months of pipeline data. For each quarter:

  Quarter: Q1 2025
  Pipeline created: $X
  Pipeline closed: $Y
  Top source: [channel]
  Notable change vs prior quarter: [1 sentence]

(format example)

Use only the data provided. If a quarter has missing fields,
write "not in source". Do not extrapolate or estimate. (what not to do)

Before producing the table, list any data quality issues you
notice. (think first)
The shortcut

When in doubt, ask yourself: "If I gave this prompt to a smart new hire on their first day, would they have enough to do the job correctly?" If not, what's missing? That missing thing goes in the prompt.

Pitfalls at this stage

  • Over-prompting. Don't write a three-page prompt for a one-line task. Match prompt length to task complexity.
  • Treating prompts as one-shot. If the first output is 80% right, follow up — don't start over.
  • Politeness theatre. "Please" and "thank you" don't help. Clear specifications do.
