Prompting basics

Five principles that separate outputs you can use from outputs you have to fix.

Most people prompt like they Google — a few words that gesture at what they want. The AI fills in the blanks with its best guess, which rarely matches what you actually needed.

Strong prompting isn't mystical. It's the same skill as writing a clear work brief: eliminate ambiguity, give context, specify what "good" looks like. The five principles below show you how.

The five principles

Principle 1 · Write a work brief, not a wish

Think of the AI as a capable contractor who just walked in the door with zero context on your company, your project, or your preferences. Everything they need to do the job well has to come from you.

Anthropic calls this the brilliant new hire test: would a smart first-day employee have everything they need from your prompt? If not, what's missing? That missing information belongs in the prompt.

A complete work brief covers these elements:

Element What to include Example
Role Who is the AI acting as? "You are a strategy analyst…"
Audience Who will read or use the output? "…for non-technical executives"
Task What exactly should it do? "Identify the two most significant trends"
Context What background does it need? "12 months of churn data, split by segment"
Format What should the output look like? "Two paragraphs, no bullet points"
Constraints Any limits on length, tone, or scope? "Under 300 words"

You don't need all six every time — but every element you leave out is a place where the AI has to guess.

Before:

Analyze our customer churn data.

After:

You are a strategy analyst helping the VP of Customer Success
prepare for a board update. Below is 12 months of monthly churn
data, broken out by segment (SMB, mid-market, enterprise).

Identify the two most significant trends. For each trend:
  - State what the data shows
  - Suggest the most plausible explanation
  - Name one question you'd want answered before drawing
    a firm conclusion

Format: two paragraphs, no bullet points. The audience is
non-technical executives.

Principle 2 · Explain the why, not just the what

The best prompts don't just describe a task — they explain why it matters. When the AI understands the purpose, it can make smart judgment calls you didn't think to specify: what to emphasize, which risks to flag, what tone to use.

A sentence of context often does more work than a whole extra paragraph of instructions.

Before:

Summarize this vendor contract.

After:

Summarize this vendor contract. I'm a first-year associate
preparing a briefing for a senior partner who needs to decide
in the next two hours whether to recommend signing.

Flag any clauses that deviate from our standard terms or that
carry material risk. Keep the summary under 300 words.

Telling the AI who the reader is and what decision they're making changes what it includes, what it emphasizes, and what it flags.

Principle 3 · Show an example — don't describe the format

If you have a specific format in mind, show it. Don't describe it.

This is called few-shot prompting in the research literature, and it's consistently one of the highest-leverage techniques. A single well-chosen example eliminates ambiguity that even a careful paragraph of instructions can't. If you have a prior output you liked — a memo, a weekly summary — paste it in as your example.

Before:

Summarize each sales call in a structured way.

After:

Summarize each sales call in the following format:

  Call: [date] with [company name]
  Stage: [prospect / active / at-risk / won / lost]
  Key concern raised: [1-2 sentences]
  Next action: [what we committed to, with owner and date]
  Risk flag: [yes/no — if yes, 1 sentence explaining why]

Example of a well-done summary:

  Call: March 14 with Thornton Logistics
  Stage: active
  Key concern raised: Their IT team needs three weeks to review
    integration requirements before they can commit.
  Next action: Jamie will send technical specs by March 17.
  Risk flag: Yes — if specs aren't approved by March 28,
    deal likely slips past quarter-end.

Principle 4 · Ask for reasoning, not just conclusions

For any analytical task, ask the AI to show its work before giving you the answer. This is called chain-of-thought prompting, and it improves accuracy on complex tasks by 20–50% in published benchmarks.

But the bigger payoff in a business context is auditability. When the AI shows its reasoning, you can spot a wrong assumption, challenge a misread, or push back on a specific step. An answer with no reasoning behind it is an answer you can't interrogate.

Before:

Based on this market data, what should our pricing strategy be?

After:

Based on the market data below, what should our pricing
strategy be?

Before giving your recommendation, work through:
  1. What does the data suggest about price sensitivity
     in each customer segment?
  2. What are the most relevant competitive reference points?
  3. What are the main trade-offs between the likely options?

Then give a recommendation. Finally, name the single assumption
that, if wrong, would most change your answer.
Remember: you're accountable

In a professional setting, you own the decisions you make with AI output — not the AI. Prompting for reasoning is how you maintain that accountability. An output you can audit is an output you can defend.

Principle 5 · Follow up — don't start over

The first output is a first draft. Don't rewrite the prompt from scratch when something's off — give targeted feedback instead. The AI keeps the full conversation context and can build on what's already working. Think of it like coaching an analyst: you'd give notes on the draft, not reassign the project.

The pattern that works: generate → critique specifically → redirect.

Weak follow-up (restarts the task):

Write a better version of the financial analysis section.

Strong follow-up (builds on what's there):

The first three paragraphs work well. The financial analysis
section is too generic — sharpen it using the actual figures
from the Q3 report I pasted above (the revenue variance table
on page 4 is especially relevant). Also cut the opening
disclaimer paragraph; the reader doesn't need it.

One more note: don't over-engineer the first prompt. A focused 80% answer that you refine in two follow-ups usually beats a three-paragraph prompt trying to anticipate everything.

Put them together

Most strong prompts use three or four of these principles at once. Here's a compound example for a strategy task:

You are a strategy associate at a consulting firm. Your client
is a mid-market SaaS company evaluating whether to expand into
the enterprise segment. I need a first-cut analysis to bring
to a partner meeting tomorrow morning — not a final recommendation,
but a structured way to frame the decision.

(Principle 1: role + audience + task defined; Principle 2: purpose stated)

Using the market data and internal metrics below, work through:
  1. What are the two strongest arguments for expansion?
  2. What are the two strongest arguments against?
  3. What are the two or three things we'd need to know to
     resolve the uncertainty?

(Principle 4: reasoning first)

Use this structure for each argument:
  Argument: [1-sentence claim]
  Evidence: [what in the data supports this]
  Caveat: [what would weaken or complicate it]

(Principle 3: format shown, not described)

[market data and internal metrics pasted below]

A strong follow-up: "The competitive analysis in argument 2 is underdeveloped — rebuild it around the margin implications in the enterprise benchmarks table." That's Principle 5 in action.

A note on grounding

These five principles are about how you structure a request. There's one more factor worth calling out: what you give the AI to work with.

AI performs significantly better when you supply source material rather than asking it to rely on general knowledge. Paste in the actual contract, the actual meeting notes, the actual data. This is called grounding — anchoring the AI's response to specific information rather than training data. It reduces hallucination and increases relevance. When precision matters, give the AI the documents. Don't ask it to guess what they say.

Pitfalls at this stage

  • Describing format instead of showing it. If you have a template or a prior example, paste it. One example beats a paragraph of formatting instructions every time.
  • Accepting the first output. It's a draft. Follow up with specific critique instead of starting over.
  • Using conclusions without checking the reasoning. For any decision that matters, ask for the reasoning. If you can't audit it, you can't responsibly act on it.
  • Skipping the why. The purpose behind a task is one of the highest-leverage things you can include. Don't treat it as optional.
  • Over-prompting simple tasks. These principles scale with complexity. A short email doesn't need a full brief — match effort to stakes.
