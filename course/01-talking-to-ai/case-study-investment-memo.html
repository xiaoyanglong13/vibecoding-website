Case study: Investment Memo

A growth equity fund asks you to draft a Series B investment memo by end of day. You have a financial model and a one-pager. Here's how domain knowledge, iteration, and verification turn a generic LLM response into a memo the partners will actually read.

Difficulty · Intermediate ~45 minutes Uses: Module 1

1. The business decision

You are a first-year associate at a growth equity fund. A partner drops a financial model and a company one-pager on your desk at 10am and asks for a draft investment memo before the 4pm investment committee meeting. The memo needs to give the partners enough to decide whether to proceed to full diligence.

The company is Meridian, a B2B SaaS platform for supply chain operations, raising a $25M Series B at a $110M post-money valuation. You have never covered this space before.

2. Data available

You have two inputs. First, a company one-pager with the following summary financials:

Company: Meridian
Founded: 2021 · HQ: Chicago · Employees: 84
Product: Supply chain operations platform (demand forecasting,
         supplier management, inventory optimization)
Customers: 47 enterprise accounts · Avg ACV: $390K
ARR: $18.4M (FY2024) · Prior year ARR: $11.0M (FY2023)
YoY ARR growth: 67%
Net Revenue Retention: 118%
Gross margin: 74%
Monthly burn: $1.2M · Runway: 14 months
Raise: $25M Series B · Post-money: ~$110M (~6x ARR)

Second, a financial model in Excel with three-year projections and a monthly P&L. You paste the summary tab into the prompt as plain text.

3. The build

Total session was about 40 minutes, broken into four rounds.

Round 1 · The vague prompt

The first attempt looked reasonable:

Write an investment memo for Meridian, a B2B SaaS company
raising a Series B.

The LLM produced five paragraphs of fluent prose. But on inspection, every sentence could apply to any SaaS company. The market sizing was invented. The risks were generic ("competition," "execution risk," "macroeconomic headwinds"). There was no recommendation. The memo would embarrass you in front of the partners.

This illustrates the core problem from page 2: when the model receives no situation — no goal, no audience, no data, no success criteria — it fills every gap with plausible-sounding assumptions. Fluent output is not the same as useful output.

Round 2 · Supplying the situation

The second prompt supplied what was missing: role, structure, audience, data, and a clear definition of success.

You are a senior associate at a growth equity fund writing
a first-draft investment memo for the investment committee.

The company is Meridian, a B2B SaaS platform for supply
chain operations (demand forecasting, supplier management,
inventory optimization). They are raising a $25M Series B
at a $110M post-money valuation (~6x ARR).

Write the memo in these sections, in this order:
1. Executive summary (3–4 sentences: what Meridian does,
   why the timing is compelling, and your recommendation)
2. Market opportunity (why supply chain software is
   attractive now; size the addressable market)
3. Business model and unit economics (use the data below;
   explain what the NRR and gross margin signal about the
   business quality)
4. Competitive positioning (2–3 specific differentiators
   vs. legacy ERP and point solutions)
5. Financial analysis (ARR trajectory, burn, path to
   profitability, use of proceeds)
6. Key risks and mitigants (3 risks specific to Meridian,
   each with one concrete mitigant)
7. Recommendation (proceed to full diligence / pass, and
   the single most important open question)

Audience: investment committee partners with 10 minutes.
They care most about: (a) why this market, why now;
(b) quality of unit economics; (c) whether the team can
execute at this scale.

Use only the data below. Flag any figure you cannot derive
from this data rather than inventing it.

[paste financials here]

This prompt is longer, but each element does specific work. The role ("senior associate") sets the register. The section structure removes ambiguity about format. The audience description tells the model what to prioritize. The instruction to flag missing data rather than invent it is essential — without it, the model will fill gaps silently.

The output was substantially better: company-specific analysis, correct figures, a clear recommendation. But it was text only, and the financial analysis section was thin.

Round 3 · Iterating toward specific outputs

The memo needed two things the partner would expect: a comparables table and a revenue progression table. Rather than rewriting the whole prompt, the next message built on what existed:

Good. Now add two elements to the financial analysis section:

1. A public-company comparables table. Pick three publicly
   traded SaaS companies with similar ACV ($200K–$500K) and
   NRR profiles (110–130%). For each, include:
   — Company name and one-line description
   — NRR
   — Gross margin
   — ARR growth (most recent fiscal year)
   — EV / ARR multiple (current)
   Then add a row for Meridian at the proposed valuation.

2. A revenue progression table showing Meridian's ARR
   trajectory across FY2023, FY2024, and the model's
   FY2025 and FY2026 projections. Include: fiscal year,
   ARR, YoY growth %, and net new ARR added.

Format both as clean tables. Label the source for any
comparable company figures you use.

This is the iterative loop from page 3 in practice: inspect the output, identify what is missing, then add context that addresses the specific gap. A follow-up of "make the financials better" would not have worked. The follow-up worked because it specified exactly what to add, what columns to include, and how to format it.

Round 4 · Verification and correction

On reading the comparables table carefully, two problems appeared. The LLM had used 124% NRR for Meridian in the table, not the 118% from the source data. It had also cited an EV/ARR multiple for one comparable that was clearly stale. Both errors were silent — the memo read confidently regardless.

This is the verify step from page 3's operating model. Every number in a memo that will go to an investment committee needs to be traced back to a source. The correction was direct:

Two corrections before we continue:

1. You used 124% NRR for Meridian in the comparables table.
   The correct figure is 118%. Please update all references
   to Meridian's NRR throughout the memo.

2. The EV/ARR multiple you cited for [Comparable Co] appears
   to be outdated. Please flag this cell as "[verify: EV/ARR
   as of current date]" rather than using a potentially
   stale figure.

After making these corrections, re-read the executive
summary and confirm that all figures there are consistent
with the corrected data.

The LLM corrected the NRR, flagged the stale comparable, and found one additional inconsistency in the executive summary on its own. The final memo was accurate and internally consistent.

4. What good looks like

The finished memo had this structure, and fit in four pages:

Section Length Key content
Executive summary 4 sentences What Meridian does, why the timing matters (supply chain disruption driving urgency), recommendation to proceed
Market opportunity 1 paragraph $18B addressable market in supply chain software, shift from ERP to purpose-built SaaS, post-pandemic urgency
Business model & unit economics 1 paragraph + table 118% NRR interpreted as net expansion signal; 74% gross margin as foundation for eventual profitability; ACV of $390K implies enterprise-grade stickiness
Competitive positioning 3 bullet points Specific differentiators vs. SAP, Oracle, and one VC-backed point solution
Financial analysis 1 paragraph + 2 tables Revenue progression table; comparables table with flagged cells; 14-month runway as the key constraint
Key risks 3 rows Sales cycle length, customer concentration (top 3 = 38% of ARR), burn rate relative to raise size — each with a specific mitigant
Recommendation 2 sentences Proceed to full diligence; most important open question is reference calls on implementation timeline

The memo reached the partner at 2:30pm. She marked it up but did not rewrite it. That is the standard.

Watch for

  • Silent hallucination of figures. The model will invent financial metrics, market sizes, and comparable company data without signaling uncertainty. Every number in a memo that will be acted on needs to be traced to a source. The instruction "flag any figure you cannot derive from this data rather than inventing it" helps, but it does not eliminate the problem — always verify manually.
  • Generic risks. Without domain context, the model defaults to "competition," "execution risk," and "macroeconomic headwinds." These are useless in an investment memo. Name the specific risk: "Customer concentration — top 3 accounts represent 38% of ARR" forces the model to reason about Meridian specifically.
  • Fluency masking thin analysis. A memo that reads well is not the same as a memo that is right. The round 1 output was grammatically impeccable and analytically empty. Inspect the reasoning, not just the prose.

5. Extensions

If you have more time, try these variations:

  • Ask the model to write a "bear case" version of the recommendation, with the same data but a skeptical prior. Compare the two memos side by side to find which risks it surfaces differently.
  • Provide a second company's financials and ask the model to compare the two investment opportunities in a single table, then recommend which to prioritize for full diligence.
  • After completing the memo, ask the model to identify the three questions a skeptical partner would most likely raise in the IC meeting, and draft brief responses to each.
