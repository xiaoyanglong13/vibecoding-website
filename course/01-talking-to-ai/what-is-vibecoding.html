What is vibecoding?

A working definition, what makes it different from regular ChatGPT use, and when it's the right tool for the job.

A working definition

Vibecoding is using AI to build software by describing what you want, in plain language, instead of writing the code yourself.

The term was popularized in early 2025 to describe a new way of working: you have an idea, you describe it to an AI, the AI writes and runs the code, you look at the result, you describe what to change, the AI updates the code. You spend your time on the what and the why; the AI handles the how.

What it isn't

Vibecoding is not the same as using ChatGPT to answer questions or write text. The key difference is that vibecoding produces working software — code that runs, files that exist on your computer, tools your colleagues can actually use. The AI isn't just describing what code would do; it's writing it, running it, fixing it when it breaks, and handing you back a finished artifact.

It's also not the same as traditional software engineering. You're not learning syntax. You're not memorizing libraries. You're not debugging line by line. You're operating at a higher level of abstraction — closer to the level you already operate at when you describe a project to a colleague or a contractor.

The contractor analogy

The most useful mental model: think of the AI as a contractor you've hired to build something for you. The contractor is fast, cheap, never sleeps, and has read everything ever written about construction. But they've never seen your house, they don't know your taste, and they'll happily build whatever you ask for — including things that don't make sense.

Your job, like any client working with a contractor, is to:

  • Describe what you want clearly, including the constraints (budget, style, what's already there).
  • Show them examples of what good looks like.
  • Check their work before signing off.
  • Know when to ask for changes versus when to rip it out and start over.
  • Recognize when the job is too big for a contractor and you need a real architect.

If you can do those five things, you can vibecode. The rest of this course is just practice.

When is vibecoding the right tool?

Vibecoding works well for:

  • One-off analyses on data you have. ("Summarize sentiment in these 400 customer reviews.")
  • Recurring reports that follow the same pattern. ("Every Monday, pull these three reports and email me the highlights.")
  • Internal tools for you or a small team. ("A page where I can paste a customer name and see their last 5 support tickets.")
  • Prototypes and validation. ("A clickable mockup of the workflow so I can show it to 10 customers.")
  • Document automation. ("Take this template and fill it in with the data from that spreadsheet.")

It works less well — and sometimes dangerously badly — for:

  • Anything customer-facing at scale. If 10,000 people will see the result, you need real engineering review.
  • Anything that handles money or PII. Mistakes get expensive and embarrassing.
  • Anything you can't verify. If you don't know what "correct" looks like, you can't tell when the AI is wrong.
  • Anything that needs to survive long-term without an engineer to maintain it.

We'll come back to this in Module 6. For now, just know that the question "should I vibecode this?" is one you should always ask, not skip.

Common confusion at this stage

  • "Isn't this just ChatGPT?" No — ChatGPT gives you text. A vibecoding tool gives you working files on your computer that you can run, share, and re-run.
  • "Don't I need to learn Python first?" No. You'll see Python (and JavaScript, and SQL) in the outputs — but you don't need to write any of it.
  • "What if it makes mistakes?" It will. Module 5 is dedicated to catching them.

Try this thought experiment

Before moving on, take 60 seconds and think of one task at work or school you'd ideally automate but assumed required engineering help. Hold onto it — by the end of Module 4 you'll know whether it's a good vibecoding candidate, and by the end of the course you'll probably have built it.
