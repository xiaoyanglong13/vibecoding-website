What is vibecoding?

A working definition, what makes it different from regular ChatGPT use, and when it's the right tool for the job.

A working definition

Vibecoding is using AI to build software by describing what you want, in plain language, instead of writing the code yourself.

The term was coined by AI researcher Andrej Karpathy, who introduced it in a February 2025 post on X:

"There's a new kind of coding I call 'vibe coding', where you fully give in to the vibes, embrace exponentials, and forget that the code even exists. … It's not really coding — I just see stuff, say stuff, run stuff, and copy paste stuff, and it mostly works."

— Andrej Karpathy, @karpathy on X, February 2, 2025

You spend your time on the what and the why; the AI handles the how.

What it isn't

Vibecoding is not the same as using ChatGPT to answer questions or write text — it produces working software that runs, not just advice about what code would do. And it's not traditional software engineering: you're not learning syntax or debugging line by line. You're operating at a higher level of abstraction, closer to describing a project to a contractor than writing the code yourself.

The contractor analogy

Your job, like any client working with a contractor, is to:

  • Describe what you want clearly, including the constraints (budget, style, what's already there).
  • Show them examples of what good looks like.
  • Check their work at the right moment — not just at the end, but at key decision points before errors compound.
  • Know when to ask for changes versus when to rip it out and start over.
  • Recognize when the job is too big to hand over in one go — and break it into smaller pieces the AI can handle reliably, or escalate to a real engineer.

The quality of what the AI builds is largely a function of how well you do these five things.

Common confusion at this stage

  • "Isn't this just ChatGPT?" No — ChatGPT gives you text. A vibecoding tool gives you working files on your computer that you can run, share, and re-run.
  • "Don't I need to learn Python first?" No. You'll see Python (and JavaScript, and SQL) in the outputs — but you don't need to write any of it.
  • "What if it makes mistakes?" It will. Module 5 is dedicated to catching them.

Try this thought experiment

Before moving on, take 60 seconds and think of one task at work or school you'd ideally automate but assumed required engineering help. Hold onto it — by the end of Module 4 you'll know whether it's a good vibecoding candidate, and by the end of the course you'll probably have built it.
